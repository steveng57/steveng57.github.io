## Background

My nephew Evan and I had been talking for a while about something I could make for his new place.  He moved in a while back and, like all new homeowners, has been going from room to room, making improvements and adding new furniture here and there.  A new TV console for the living room was in order.  Something was definitely needed to replace the Home Depot plank-and-cinder-block contraption that I had put together for him as an emergency setup when he first moved in.

{% include html-side.html img="4884155735830755613.avif" align="left" %}

### An Internet Search

After scouring the Internet, Evan found a couple of designs that he liked, and we eventually settled on this one.  

I love the fact that it has two different wood types; it gives the piece a lot of interest.

It has an outer carcass made of walnut, giving it a nice, warm brown vibe.  The inner carcass is made of stained wood—I'm not sure what kind is shown in the picture—but I chose red oak for my version.

## The Build

Next came the construction.  This was probably one of the longer projects I have done, partially because it was complex, partially because it involved some things I had never tried before, and partially because there was a setback or two along the way.  This is called "foreshadowing."

{% include html-side.html img="IMG_2388.avif" align="left" %}

### Standard Red Oak

I had some red oak in my timber collection, so I used that.  The nice thing about using existing stock is that you don't have to wait for it to dry out or anything.

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

{% include html-side.html img="IMG_2396.avif" align="right" %}

### Oak Panels

The way I like to make panels is to make them as wide as I can, leave the boards a little thicker than the final spec, and send them through the planer after the glue has dried.  This saves a lot of sanding and makes the final product super smooth.

Unfortunately, the final width of these panels was about 50 cm (20 inches), which is too wide for my planer, so I had to do them in groups.

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

{% include html-side.html img="IMG_2398.avif" align="left" %}

### Top and Sides

It can be a little tricky to glue things up when there is no supporting piece on the bottom to apply pressure to, but it worked.

The inner carcass top and sides are assembled in this pic.

### Stretchers and Dividers

Trying to keep the weight down, I opted for four stretchers on the inner carcass, two on the bottom and two on the back.

Then the drawer dividers were installed by mounting them to the oak top and the bottom stretchers.

The front stretcher on the bottom and the drawer divider had to be edge-banded with custom (homemade) red oak bands.  Although not easily visible, these pieces can be seen when the drawers are open.  

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

### Arrrrrgggggghhhh...

> **WARNING:** The following images may be disturbing to some woodworkers.  Reader discretion is advised.
{: .prompt-warning .sjg-br}

{% include html-sxs.html img1="IMG_2430.avif"  img2="IMG_2431.avif" %}

This was a crazy part of the project: frustration after frustration after frustration.  Let me explain.

The outer carcass in the design calls for walnut.  If I were to try to make this out of solid walnut, it would be so heavy as to be almost immovable.  Also, over time a plank like that could warp or bend, as there really isn't anything to keep it in line.

So what I set out to do was make a plywood interior with a hollow core, then add some walnut veneer on the outside.  I thought I would try to make my own walnut veneers, some of which would be over 1.5 metres long.  This would give it the nice walnut finish, but with the strength and stability of a plywood core.  That did not work out as planned.

I tried putting many pieces through the bandsaw, but something was always wrong with them.  The YouTube guys make this look easy, but they take an 18-inch piece and run it through with no problems.  I can get an 18-inch run done with no issues, but these were 60 inches long.  Out of about 25 veneers that I made, I think three turned out to be acceptable: the colouring on some was bad, there were knots in the wrong places, and lastly, the veneers were cut too thin in at least one spot along their length.  I can work with "too thick," but "too thin" is tough to fix.

I am not sure what was going wrong; either my bandsaw needs tuning up or I need more practice.  I decided to cool down, put this on hold, and work on another part of the project.

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

### Drawer Boxes

### Baltic Birch Boxes

Switching to the drawer boxes, I built them in my standard way, with Dominos at the joints.  I used 15 mm Baltic birch plywood, which is super strong and stable, so I edge-banded the top of each box to hide the plywood.

Interestingly, I see a lot more professional products these days that just let the plywood show through.  It probably saves them some money, but as long as it is good-quality plywood, I think it looks great.  I might try that on future projects.

### Pulls

The drawer pulls are another rewarding milestone.  I love these ultra-wide pulls—solid, with contemporary class.  

I really had to double-check the alignment on these and get them right on all three drawer fronts.  The drawer fronts are _"continuous grain"_, meaning all three are cut from a single panel so that the grain flows seamlessly from one end to the other.  If I screw up badly on any one of them, I can't just remake that one; I have to remake all three.  

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

### The Walnut

Okay, back to the outer carcass and the walnut panels.  I originally planned to make my own long veneers, but that plan went wonky, so I decided on another approach.  I found some high-quality walnut plywood at my favourite wood supplier, {{ site.data.links.kjp }}, where my guy Jeff had acquired some double-thick veneered plywood.  It was just what this called for, since the problem with regular veneered plywood is that if you sand through it, you are in real trouble.  The extra thickness helps prevent that.  It was pricey but worth it.

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

{% include html-sxs.html img2="IMG_2816.avif" img1="IMG_2817.avif" %}

### In the Bag

So I had to laminate three pieces of plywood together: a top layer of walnut, a middle core of Baltic birch, and a bottom layer of walnut.

This was kind of tricky, as it had to be done right.  If I just put a bunch of weights on it, it might end up lumpy and bumpy.  Not good.

So I decided to try the vacuum-press method.  I had seen this in my travels, and it was the perfect approach for this type of work, but I had never done it before, so it was a bit of an experiment.

### Wrap It Up

What you have to do is put whatever you are laminating into an airtight bag, with a _platen_ board on the top and another on the bottom.  

A _platen_ is a board with airways cut into it to let the air flow around your project, and it also serves to protect the bag from any glue that might squeeze out.

For my five-layer sandwich, this large zippered bag was the perfect fit.

### Pump the Jam

Once it is all set up and nicely aligned in the bag, you attach the pump, fire it up, and let it do its thing.

This creates an even pressure of 25 psi over the entire surface of the workpiece.  No lumpy bumpy.

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

## Finishing

Now it was time for the finish.  I used my standard {{ site.data.links.rubio }} for this project, but with a couple of new variants I had never tried before.  The Urban Grey was a nice new addition, and the SheenPlus should give the finish a slightly satin look.  Let's find out.

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

{% include html-side.html img="IMG_2906.avif" align="left" %}

### Precolor Easy

In this mix, I used a 50/50 split of Intense Black with Urban Grey for the precolor stain.  It came out a nice charcoal grey.

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

{% include html-side.html img="IMG_2917.avif" align="right" %}

### Satin Walnut

The walnut outer carcass was not stained; that would be a horrible thing to do to walnut.  

Here we let the natural colour and grain of the walnut shine through.  The Pure colour of the hard-wax oil makes them both pop, with a little added sheen.

{% include clear-float.html break=1 %}

{% include html-side.html img="IMG_2922.avif" align="left" %}

### Charcoal Oak

For the red oak inner carcass, which was stained charcoal grey in a previous step, we used a charcoal-grey-coloured oil for the first coat, then just the pure sheen for the second coat.
